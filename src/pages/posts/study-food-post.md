# Introduction

So, I'm sure we've all seen everyone's hot new take on how to eat before an exam--everything from carb overloads to caffeine sprees--and I'm going to be really honest, there's no one food or eating method that is universally effective. However, there are still *categories* of food that can be effective, and those categories are the topics that I will be discussing in this article.

With that said, let's begin!

# It All Starts In The Gut

Now, there's no way to get around this--eating healthy *will help* to improve your cognitive function. Ultra-processed foods are not only bad for your body's health, but they're bad for your mind, too. They're loaded with *processed* sugar and carbs.

But the brain runs on sugar and carbs, right? So why is junk food bad?

The problem isn't the carb itself, it's the package it comes in. Between two carbs--for example a potato you just baked at home and a bag of potato chips--which one is healthier? The baked potato, of course. But they're both carbs (or *starches*), and they're both potatoes. The difference is the way in which they're made. The baked potato was made at home, and even when you load it up with toppings it's still healthier because you know exactly what went into it, and the most processing it received was from washing, cutting, and then baking.

The potato chips on the other hand have been through *much* more, and on top of that they're designed to *not* be filling, so that you want to eat more and more of them.

The point is, eating healthy doesn't mean you have to give up junk food and become a salad guru, but it *does* mean that you should cook more, and try to at least limit fast food, especially on weeks where you have a big test coming up and you need to study.

So what foods *are* good for studying?

## Fatty Fish

Fish, particularly fatty fish, is a much-overlooked part of a healthy diet. The very phrase, fatty fish, probably doesn't conjure the mental image of health. I know it still doesn't for me, and that's *knowing* that it's a good food. Here's the thing--fatty fish, such as salmon, is delicious, and it doesn't really conjure an unhealthy image in your head, does it?

These so-called 'fatty fish' are nutritious because they contain an *unsaturated fat* called omega-3. Omega-3 is an amazing nutrient for improving brain health and function, and there is even a Harvard study out there linking fatty fish with reduced cognitive decline later in life!

If you're not a fish fan, though, you're not doomed! You can substitute fish for flax seeds, avocados, and walnuts, which also contain omega-3.

With that said, let's move on to the next food topic--the dreaded vegetables.

## Leafy Greens

Dark greens, such as kale, spinach, and cruciferous veggies like broccoli, are rich with nutrients, like vitamin K, lutein, and a bunch of other names that would make this article much harder to read quickly, so I'll skip over them for now--but I definitely recommend you research them!

Now, I'm not saying you have to eat salads all the time--and definitely don't eat straight lettuce, because that frankly won't taste very good. Try different methods of cooking your vegetables! Saute the kale in butter, or make spinach omelettes! You could even try roasting some veggies with oil and herbs! Anything to make the vegetables more appealing to yourself.

Now that we've gotten veggies out of the way, let's move on to snacks.

## Fruits & Nuts

Fruits and nuts make the perfect study snack foods, unless of course you're allergic to them, in which case I recommend not eating them. However, if you're not, definitely eat these as a snack instead of candy or chips. There are so many different kinds of fruits out there, and they're all healthy in their own way--they can provide energy and antioxidants, and they're my go-to if I'm craving something sweet but don't want to undermine myself. 

As for nuts, they're also incredibly healthy--they're rich in proteins and sometimes fatty acids (such as omega-3). They provide energy, and are good at filling your stomach so you don't feel the need to snack forever. If you're like me, though, and you're not a huge fan of actual whole nuts, I recommend getting unsweetened peanut butter or something kind of like that. Make sure it's unsweetened, though, and not too processed! My favorite thing to do when I'm craving a snack is to cut up an apple or something like that, and eat it with 1-2 tablespoons of peanut or almond butter. It's filling, delicious, and it improves my mood, too.

And now, without further ado, let's move on to the next section!

## Sweets & Caffeine

Now, I don't know about you, dear reader, but I have a serious addiction to coffee and chocolate, and I'm not giving that up! I do try to moderate it, though. See the thing is, even though chocolate and other candies are heavily processed, there are types of sweets that are somewhat healthier than others--dark chocolate is one of them! Good dark chocolate is actually rich in something called flavonoids, which can help to improve bloodflow to the brain and speed up thinking! 

So, here's my final take on sugary things--as long as you eat your sugar in moderation, you likely won't feel any adverse effects.

As for caffeine, this one is trickier. For some people, it's like the magic energy boost, for others it can cause anxiety and jitters, and if you're like me, it might not do anything at all and you just like the flavor. But for some, particularly people with heart issues, it can be dangerous because it can speed up their pulse. So, I won't advocate either way on the idea of caffeinated coffees and teas--that is entirely up to you, and the research you do.

If you're not sure about caffeine, I would say just stick with *water*. In fact, even if you do drink coffee and tea, drink water as well! Dehydration is a *significant* cause of moodiness and brain fog, and you should aim to drink plenty of water throughout the day.

# Closing
